I own two of these bags. A field-style shoulder bag I use for scouting trips and a larger duffel that's ridden in the back of my truck for years now, through mud, snow, and more than a few times getting rained on and left there overnight because I forgot to bring it in. Neither one has a broken zipper, a delaminated strap, or canvas that's gone soft and thin the way cheap bags do after a couple seasons. The leather has actually gotten better — darker, more supple, the kind of patina you can't buy new no matter what a catalog tells you.

That's the whole review, honestly. But let's get into why.

## The construction is why it survives

Bob Timberlake bags were built with 18oz cotton twill canvas, which is heavy enough to shrug off brush and abrasion without a lot of babying, and it carries real DWR water resistance — not the marketing kind that washes out after a season, the kind that still beads water after years of use. The leather trim is top-grain, oiled at the tannery, which is why it ages instead of cracking. And the hardware is the part most bags cheap out on: these use solid brass buckles and clasps, and real YKK zippers. Zippers are almost always the first thing to go on a bag — the pull breaks, the teeth stop meeting, the slider wears loose. On both of my bags, after years of use, the zippers still run clean.

Compare that to what $250 to $400 buys you new right now in a similar "heritage canvas" bag, and you're often getting synthetic-coated canvas, bonded leather trim, and a zipper that's fine for the first year. You're paying a premium for the look of quality, not the material itself.

## Where to actually find them

These aren't in production anymore, so you're buying secondhand — eBay and Poshmark are where I've had the most luck. Search "Bob Timberlake canvas leather bag" and be patient; new listings show up regularly because these were made in real numbers back when they were sold through catalogs and outfitters. Expect to pay anywhere from $15 for a smaller accessory bag up to $60 for a full-size duffel or briefcase, depending on size and condition. That's not a typo. I've watched clean examples sell for less than a nice dinner out.

## What to check before you buy

Look closely at the listing photos. The leather should look flexible in the photos, not stiff or spider-webbed with cracks — cracked leather means it dried out and wasn't cared for, and it won't come back. Ask the seller (or zoom into photos) for a clear shot of the zipper pull and ask if it runs smoothly end to end; a sticky or misaligned zipper is the one flaw that's genuinely annoying to live with. And check the canvas for mildew spotting or heavy staining, especially on bags that may have sat in a damp closet or garage for a decade — surface dirt cleans up fine, but mildew smell rarely fully goes away.

Someone paid full retail for this bag twenty or thirty years ago. It outlasted their ownership of it, and now it's sitting on a resale site for less than a tank of gas, ready to outlast yours too.
