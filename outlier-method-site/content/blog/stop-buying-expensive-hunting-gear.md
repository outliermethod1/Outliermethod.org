Walk into any outdoor retailer or scroll any hunting brand's site and you'll find technical jackets running $300 to $500 retail. Every single time I look at what's actually inside one of those jackets — the fabric, the stitching, the hardware — I can't find $400 worth of material. What I find is a logo, a marketing budget, and a price tag built to justify both. You are not paying for superior weather protection. You are paying for the privilege of wearing a brand name into the woods.

Here's the part that should make you angry: somebody already solved this problem, decades ago, and then we all forgot about it. American wool and canvas outerwear built from the 1940s through the 1980s wasn't made to be replaced every few seasons. It was made because a company's entire reputation depended on a coat surviving a man's actual working life — checking trap lines, working timber, sitting in a duck blind at four in the morning in February. "Built to last generations" wasn't a slogan then. It was the only acceptable standard, because customers wouldn't tolerate anything less and neither would the company selling it to them. That standard is still sitting in closets and on secondhand racks all over the country, and almost nobody is bidding on it.

## Where to actually look, and what you'll pay

**Johnson Woolen Mills** — Vermont-made, 100% wool, brass hardware, genuinely built for hard field use. This is about as close to the platonic ideal of American hunting wool as it gets, and the company is still around, which tells you something about how they built things the first time. You'll find worn workhorse pieces on Poshmark and eBay from around $25, and pristine rare vintage runs $150 to $400. Don't chase pristine. A Johnson coat with honest wear on it has already proven the only thing that matters — that it survives real use — and it'll keep proving it for you.

**Sears Fieldmaster** — the classic American mail-order outdoor line, heavy wool and canvas construction, built for a customer base that couldn't afford to replace gear every season and wasn't going to. Often findable for $30 to $60. Nobody's paying a premium for the name anymore, which is exactly why it's a bargain now.

**Duxbak** — a genuinely historic dedicated hunting brand, canvas duck and wool construction, built specifically for the field rather than adapted from general workwear. Findable for $20 to $100. This is the "if you know, you know" find — most people scrolling past a Duxbak listing have no idea what they're looking at, which is precisely why it's still cheap.

**Eddie Bauer vintage down** — their legendary vintage down parkas and jackets, the old Kara Koram-era pieces in particular, are a well-known and genuinely well-insulated secondhand market. Eddie Bauer basically pioneered down outerwear in America, and the old pieces are still warmer per ounce than a lot of what's on shelves today.

**Levi's and Lee vintage denim and canvas workwear** — not glamorous, but built tough and everywhere secondhand for cheap. A vintage duck canvas chore coat or a heavyweight denim jacket will outlast three seasons of something synthetic, and you'll pay a fraction of the price.

**Carhartt** — worth a mention on reputation alone. Their durability track record is well-earned, and older pieces show up secondhand constantly at a steep discount to new.

## How to actually shop this

Search the brand name plus "hunting coat," "field jacket," or "canvas coat" on eBay and Poshmark. Look closely at every listing photo before you buy — zoom in on the wool for moth holes, check that buttons and buckles are present and not cracked, and look at seams for separation. Sellers who care will show you close-ups; sellers who don't, ask. And get your actual chest and sleeve measurements before you shop, not your modern jacket size — vintage sizing runs differently across decades and brands, and a size that fits you in a current catalog can be badly wrong in a coat cut fifty years ago. Measure a jacket you already own that fits the way you want this one to fit, and shop against those numbers.

## The buy-it-used math, explained plainly

Somebody already ate the depreciation on this jacket. Whatever it cost new, whoever bought it first absorbed that hit, and it doesn't touch your wallet at all. The materials aren't a gamble — they're proven, by decades of actual field use, in a way no new jacket can claim no matter what the tag says. And the money you didn't spend — often $200 to $400 per jacket, compared to a comparable new technical piece — goes toward things that actually deserve a new-price tag: your tags, your hunt itself, or gear where "new" genuinely matters, like optics or boots you need to break in yourself from day one. Buying old on the outerwear frees up real money for the parts of a hunt that can't be bought secondhand.

## The Outlier take

This is the entire thesis of Start Smart, distilled into one coat-shaped example: proven and earned beats new and hyped, every single time. The industry wants you to believe good gear has to be new gear. It doesn't. It has to actually work, and the best proof that something works is that it already has — for forty winters, on somebody else's dime.

Buy it earned, not overspent.
