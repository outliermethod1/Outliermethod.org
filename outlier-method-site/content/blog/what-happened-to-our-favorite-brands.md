There's a particular kind of trust that builds up around a brand name over generations. Your father wore it, maybe your grandfather did too, and the name on the tag meant something specific and dependable — this coat will keep you warm, this boot will hold up, this company stands behind what it sells. That trust doesn't happen by accident, and it doesn't survive by accident either. It has to keep being earned, year after year, decision after decision. Lately, a few of the names that earned it hardest have been spending it down. I want to walk through exactly what happened, because the facts matter more than the feeling, even though the feeling is real too.

## Eddie Bauer

Eddie Bauer opened its doors in Seattle in 1920, and this wasn't a company that coasted on marketing. In 1940, Eddie Bauer himself patented the first quilted down jacket made in America — an actual innovation, not a rebrand of somebody else's idea. The company went on to outfit U.S. Army Air Force pilots with cold-weather flight gear in World War II, and equipped Jim Whittaker, the first American to summit Everest. That's not brand mythology. That's the honest record of a company that built genuinely field-tested gear when the stakes were literally life and death.

Over the following decades, ownership changed hands repeatedly — General Mills, Spiegel, Golden Gate Capital, and others across the years — and with each change, more of the manufacturing moved overseas, to China, Vietnam, and elsewhere. And in 2026, Eddie Bauer LLC filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy and closed all of its North American retail stores. That's not a rumor or an old complaint recycled online. That happened this year, to a company that once outfitted the first American on top of the tallest mountain in the world.

## Filson

Filson's flagship piece, the Mackinaw Cruiser, was built in Seattle using wool sourced from Pendleton, Oregon — American wool, milled in America, sewn into a coat in America. Somewhere along the way, that changed. Filson's wool sourcing shifted to an Italian supplier, and some of their premium wool coats moved to being manufactured in Vietnam. Meanwhile, prices kept climbing the whole time. Longtime customers noticed, and they said so — outdoor forums and gear communities have specifically called out this shift as the moment Filson stopped being worth what it costs. When the wool isn't from Pendleton and the coat isn't sewn where the company is headquartered, you're not paying for the same thing your father paid for, even if the label looks identical.

## L.L. Bean

I want to be fair here, because L.L. Bean's story isn't as clean-cut as the other two, and honesty matters more than a tidy narrative. L.L. Bean still manufactures their signature Boots and their Boat and Tote bags in Maine, at their own facilities, and that's genuinely worth crediting — a lot of companies would have moved that production overseas decades ago, and L.L. Bean didn't. That's real, and it counts.

But the rest of their catalog tells a more familiar story. Much of L.L. Bean's broader product line moved to overseas manufacturing, including production in China. And in 2018, they discontinued their famous lifetime warranty — the promise that if a product ever failed you, for any reason, at any point in its life, they'd make it right. That warranty was the clearest possible signal of a company's confidence in what it made. Ending it is a real signal too, just the opposite kind.

## Why this happens

None of this means everything these companies sell now is bad, and I'm not going to stand here and tell you the people at Eddie Bauer or Filson or L.L. Bean don't care. That's not really what's going on. What's going on is simpler and more familiar than that: cutting manufacturing costs looks good on a spreadsheet. It improves margins this quarter, it pleases whoever owns the company this year, and the damage it does to the brand's reputation happens slowly enough that nobody has to answer for it right away. Piece by piece, the promise these brands were built on — made here, built to last, backed forever — gets traded away for numbers that look better in a boardroom. It's not malice. It's incentives, working exactly the way incentives work, and the customer is the one who eventually pays for it in a coat that doesn't hold up the way the old one did.

## The Outlier take

This is exactly why vintage and secondhand matters so much to how we think about gear here. A 1970s Filson coat, sewn in Seattle from real Pendleton wool, holds more integrity in its stitching than a lot of what's sold new under that same name today. The old Eddie Bauer down pieces, made back when the company was still innovating instead of just licensing a legacy name, will outwarm and outlast plenty of what's on shelves right now. Buying that old coat isn't nostalgia. It's just correctly recognizing which version of the brand actually earned your trust — and it's the same case we made in [Stop Buying Expensive Hunting Gear](/blog/stop-buying-expensive-hunting-gear), if you want the fuller argument for buying it old instead of new.

The name on the tag used to be a promise. Go find the coats that were made back when these companies still kept it.
